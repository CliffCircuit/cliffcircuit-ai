BPC-157 (Body Protection Compound 157)

BPC-157 (Body Protection Compound 157) — Mavyn Rep Training Sheet

Category: Tissue Repair / GI Healing / Neuroprotective Peptide | (Component of KLOW & GLOW Stacks)

1. What Is BPC-157?

BPC-157 (Body Protection Compound-157) is a synthetic pentadecapeptide (15 amino acids: GEPPPGKPADDAGLV) derived from a protective protein found in human gastric juice. It was discovered and extensively studied by Dr. Predrag Sikiric and his team at the University of Zagreb, Croatia, who have published over 150 peer-reviewed papers on the compound.

BPC-157 is stable in gastric acid (unlike most peptides) because of its origin in the gastric environment — it was literally discovered in the gastric protective protein fraction. This stability makes both oral and injectable routes viable.

Mechanism of Action (Molecular/Cellular Level)

Nitric Oxide (NO) System Modulation:

BPC-157 is one of the most potent modulators of the nitric oxide system discovered. It activates eNOS (endothelial nitric oxide synthase) via the FAK-paxillin pathway, producing highly localized NO at injury sites. This:

  • Promotes angiogenesis (new blood vessel formation at wound sites)
  • Vasodilates local blood supply to injured tissues
  • Drives fibroblast migration and proliferation
  • Activates local stem cell recruitment

FAK-paxillin Pathway Activation:

BPC-157 directly phosphorylates Focal Adhesion Kinase (FAK) and paxillin — cytoskeletal signaling proteins that regulate cell migration, proliferation, and extracellular matrix attachment. This is the primary mechanism by which BPC-157 accelerates tissue healing at virtually every site it reaches.

Tendon Healing — Tendon-to-Bone Integration:

BPC-157 upregulates both VEGFR2 (vascular endothelial growth factor receptor 2) and FGFR2 (fibroblast growth factor receptor 2) in tenocytes and tendon fibroblasts — the two most critical growth factor signaling pathways for tendon repair. Studies specifically show enhanced collagen fiber organization, improved tendon-to-bone insertion strength, and faster functional recovery.

Gut Healing (Cytoprotective):

In the GI tract, BPC-157:

  • Stimulates growth of intestinal villus epithelium
  • Increases expression of tight junction proteins (claudin, occludin, ZO-1) — repairing "leaky gut"
  • Suppresses gastric acid hypersecretion
  • Promotes ulcer healing by stimulating mucosal blood flow
  • Activates enteric nervous system (ENS) repair

Nerve Regeneration:

BPC-157 promotes peripheral nerve regeneration by stimulating Schwann cell activity, axonal sprouting, and remyelination. In crush injury models, functional nerve recovery is dramatically accelerated. Also promotes neurogenesis in the CNS and has antidepressant-like effects via dopamine system modulation.

Dopamine & Serotonin System Repair:

Uniquely, BPC-157 modulates both dopaminergic and serotonergic transmission — it can reverse or mitigate drug-induced neurotoxicity (amphetamine, cocaine damage), and has demonstrated antidepressant and anxiolytic properties in animal models. This is unrelated to its tissue repair properties and is an independent mechanism.

Anti-Inflammatory: Potently suppresses COX-2 and NF-κB, reducing PGE2 and inflammatory cytokines. However — like TB-500 — it is "pro-healing" rather than purely anti-inflammatory; it directs inflammation toward productive healing rather than simply suppressing it.

10. FAQ — For Rep Use

Q: Can BPC-157 be taken orally?

A: Yes — uniquely so among most peptides. BPC-157 is stable in gastric acid (it was derived from a gastric protein) and oral capsules are effective for gut conditions. For systemic/musculoskeletal, SubQ injection provides better distribution.

Q: How does BPC-157 work so differently on so many tissues?

A: The FAK-paxillin and NO/VEGF pathways it activates are fundamental to cellular repair in ALL tissue types — not just specific organs. When you activate these healing pathways, every tissue that has them (essentially all of them) responds. BPC-157 is a master regulator of the healing cascade.

Q: Is it safe for the gut long-term?

A: For IBD and chronic GI conditions, long-term use appears safe based on extensive animal data and clinical case series. No adverse GI events have been documented — in fact, it consistently protects GI tissue.

Q: How does BPC-157 compare to prolotherapy or PRP?

A: These are different mechanisms. PRP (platelet-rich plasma) releases growth factors locally from concentrated platelets. BPC-157 activates FAK/VEGF healing signaling pathways directly and has the advantage of systemic reach, oral delivery option, and superior research depth.

Q: Why isn't it FDA approved yet?

A: The research has been dominated by academic groups (primarily Dr. Sikiric's lab) rather than pharmaceutical companies funding expensive Phase 3 RCTs. The compound cannot be easily patented, reducing pharmaceutical investment incentive. The science is robust; the regulatory pathway simply hasn't been completed by a commercial sponsor.

TB-500 (Thymosin Beta-4)

TB-500 (Thymosin Beta-4) — Mavyn Rep Training Sheet

SKUs: TB5 (5mg) | TB10 (10mg) | Category: Tissue Repair / Recovery Peptide

1. What Is TB-500?

TB-500 is a synthetic version of the active fragment of Thymosin Beta-4 (Tβ4), a naturally occurring 43-amino acid protein found in virtually all human and animal cells. The specific fragment used is the actin-binding domain: Ac-LKKTETQ (residues 17–23), which retains the full biological activity of the parent molecule.

Thymosin Beta-4 was originally isolated from thymosin fraction 5 — a thymic extract used in immunological research. It is one of the most abundant intracellular peptides in mammals, with extraordinarily high concentrations in platelets, wound fluid, and regenerating tissue.

Mechanism of Action (Molecular/Cellular Level)

Actin Sequestration & Cell Motility: TB-500's primary function is binding G-actin (monomeric actin) and preventing its polymerization into F-actin filaments. This maintains a pool of mobile actin monomers available for rapid cell migration. During tissue injury, this dramatically accelerates:

  • Stem cell migration to injury sites
  • Endothelial cell migration (angiogenesis)
  • Keratinocyte migration (wound closure)
  • Satellite cell activation in muscle

Angiogenesis (New Blood Vessel Formation): TB-500 strongly upregulates vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) receptor expression and promotes the formation of new capillary networks at injury sites. Better vascularization = faster delivery of oxygen, nutrients, and immune cells to damaged tissue.

Anti-Inflammatory Action: TB-500 downregulates NF-κB signaling, reducing production of TNF-α, IL-1β, and IL-6. Critically, it reduces inflammatory cell infiltration at injury sites without suppressing the healing phase of inflammation — it is anti-inflammatory but pro-healing.

MMP Regulation: Modulates matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs) to facilitate extracellular matrix remodeling — breaking down damaged collagen/fibrin while stimulating organized new collagen deposition. This reduces scar formation and improves tissue quality.

Stem Cell Activation: Promotes migration and differentiation of CD34+ progenitor cells and cardiac stem cells. In cardiac injury models, TB-500 has stimulated cardiomyocyte regeneration — a highly novel finding.

Satellite Cell Activation (Muscle): Directly activates muscle satellite cells (myogenic progenitors) to proliferate and fuse with damaged muscle fibers, accelerating fiber repair and hypertrophy.

Neuroregeneration: TB-500 promotes axonal sprouting, remyelination, and oligodendrocyte survival — making it relevant for nerve injury, TBI, and spinal cord damage.

  • Activates quiescent satellite cells to re-enter the cell cycle
  • Promotes satellite cell self-renewal
  • Drives satellite cell differentiation and fusion into existing muscle fibers
  • Increases myonuclear density (more nuclei = higher protein synthetic capacity = more muscle ceiling)

Anti-Apoptotic:

IGF-1R/Akt signaling phosphorylates and inactivates pro-apoptotic proteins (Bad, caspase-9) and activates anti-apoptotic proteins (Bcl-2, Bcl-XL). In neurons, this is neuroprotective; in muscle, it prevents exercise-induced cell death.

Insulin-Like Metabolic Effects:

IGF-1 binds the insulin receptor weakly (shared IR/IGF-1R structure), contributing to:

  • Glucose uptake in muscle (similar to insulin but less potent)
  • Enhanced amino acid uptake for protein synthesis
  • Lipogenesis modulation

Note: This creates hypoglycemia risk — a key safety consideration for IGF-1 LR3 administration.

Organ and Tissue Growth:

IGF-1 stimulates growth in virtually all tissues: bone length/density, cartilage, organs (thyroid, pancreas, heart, brain). It is a true growth hormone effector — the molecule that actually executes GH's growth signals.

1. What Is HGH?

Human Growth Hormone (HGH), also known as somatotropin, is a 191-amino acid single-chain polypeptide hormone produced and secreted by somatotroph cells in the anterior pituitary gland. It is one of the most important hormones in the human body, regulating growth during childhood and continuing to influence metabolism, body composition, cardiovascular function, bone density, cognition, and recovery throughout adult life.

Recombinant Human Growth Hormone (rhGH) is bio-identical to the endogenous hormone, produced via recombinant DNA technology in E. coli or mammalian cell cultures. It is an FDA-approved pharmaceutical (Genotropin, Norditropin, Saizen, Humatrope) for multiple indications.

GH secretion declines dramatically with age:

  • Peak GH secretion: adolescence
  • Age 20 → 60: ~50–75% decline in daily GH output
  • After age 60: further decline; some adults produce minimal GH

This decline (somatopause) is a major driver of age-related body composition changes, fatigue, decreased bone density, and metabolic dysfunction.

Mechanism of Action (Molecular/Cellular Level)

Growth Hormone Receptor (GHR) Signaling:

GH binds to the GHR (a class I cytokine receptor) at target tissues. The primary signaling cascade:

  • GH → dimerizes GHR → activates JAK2 (Janus kinase 2) → phosphorylates STAT5b → nuclear translocation → IGF-1 gene transcription

Hepatic IGF-1 Production:

The liver is the primary site of GH action for systemic IGF-1 production. ~70% of circulating IGF-1 comes from GH-stimulated hepatic synthesis. IGF-1 then circulates bound to IGFBPs (binding proteins) and acts on essentially every cell type (see IGF-1 LR3 training sheet for IGF-1 mechanisms).

Direct GH Effects (IGF-1 Independent):

GH also acts directly on tissues without requiring IGF-1:

  • Adipose tissue: Direct lipolytic effect — activates hormone-sensitive lipase → triglyceride hydrolysis → free fatty acid release → energy substrate
  • Liver: Modulates glucose metabolism, promotes gluconeogenesis
  • Bone: Direct effect on periosteum; IGF-1-dependent longitudinal growth
  • Muscle: Direct protein synthesis stimulation partially independent of IGF-1

Anti-Insulin / Diabetogenic Effect:

GH acutely reduces insulin sensitivity (especially at pharmacological doses) — this is a key dose-management consideration. The GH → IGF-1 axis is overall insulin-sensitizing long-term, but acute high GH levels impair insulin signaling.

Pulsatile Release Physiology:

Natural GH is released in pulses — primarily during:

1. Deep slow-wave sleep (largest pulse of the day)

2. Exercise-induced pulse

3. Fasting/hypoglycemia-induced pulse

Exogenous rhGH replaces this pulsatile pattern with a constant baseline, which has different physiological effects. This is why GH secretagogues (Ipamorelin, CJC-1295) are often preferred for preserving pulse physiology.

10. FAQ — For Rep Use

Q: Is HGH a steroid?

A: No. Epiphyseal growth plates close after puberty; height growth stops. In adults, HGH affects body composition, organ health, and metabolic function — not bone elongation.

A: IGF-1 blood levels are the gold standard measurement. A baseline IGF-1 test before starting + repeat at 6–8 weeks shows whether your protocol is producing the target IGF-1 increase. Q: What happens when you stop?

A: GH effects reverse gradually — body fat redistributes back, muscle gains partly reverse. The pituitary's own GH production recovers (usually within weeks). 1. What Is NAD+?

NAD+ (Nicotinamide Adenine Dinucleotide) is a coenzyme found in every living cell — arguably the most fundamental molecule in cellular energy metabolism. It exists in two forms: NAD+ (oxidized) and NADH (reduced), cycling between them as it shuttles electrons in metabolic reactions.

NAD+ levels peak in youth and decline 40–60% between ages 20 and 60. This decline is increasingly recognized as a central driver of aging, metabolic dysfunction, and age-related disease. Restoring NAD+ levels is one of the most evidence-backed longevity interventions in modern geroscience.

Mechanism of Action (Molecular/Cellular Level)

Mitochondrial Energy Production (Primary):

NAD+ is the essential cofactor for the electron transport chain (ETC). In every cell, NAD+ accepts electrons from glycolysis and the TCA cycle to become NADH, which then donates electrons to Complex I of the mitochondrial ETC → generating ATP. Without NAD+, cells cannot produce energy efficiently — every organ system suffers.

Sirtuin Activation (Sirtuins 1–7):

Sirtuins are NAD+-dependent deacetylases — the "longevity enzymes." They require NAD+ as a substrate (not just a cofactor). Key functions:

  • SIRT1: Gene expression regulation, autophagy induction, mitochondrial biogenesis (via PGC-1α)
  • SIRT3: Mitochondrial protein deacetylation, ROS reduction, metabolic efficiency
  • SIRT6: DNA repair, genomic stability, telomere maintenance
  • SIRT7: Chromatin remodeling, stress response

As NAD+ declines with age, sirtuin activity declines — accelerating the hallmarks of aging: genomic instability, telomere attrition, mitochondrial dysfunction, cellular senescence.

PARP Activation (DNA Repair):

PARP-1 (Poly ADP-ribose polymerase) is the primary DNA repair enzyme in the nucleus. It consumes enormous amounts of NAD+ during DNA repair. Aging, UV radiation, inflammation, and oxidative stress cause persistent DNA damage, chronically depleting NAD+ through PARP hyperactivation — creating a vicious cycle of NAD+ depletion → impaired repair → more damage.

CD38 — NAD+ Consumer:

CD38 is an enzyme that degrades NAD+ to cyclic ADP-ribose (cADPR). CD38 expression increases dramatically with age and inflammation. It is responsible for a large portion of age-related NAD+ decline. This is why precursor supplementation must overcome the CD38 drain.

NMN/NR Pathway:

Intravenous/subcutaneous NAD+ bypasses the oral bioavailability issue — oral NAD+ is largely cleaved to precursors in the gut. Direct NAD+ injection provides immediate cellular replenishment without conversion steps, achieving 10–100x higher bioavailability than oral NMN or NR.

1. What Is MOTS-c?

MOTS-c (Mitochondrial Open Reading Frame of the 12S rRNA-c) is a mitochondria-derived peptide (MDP) — a revolutionary class of signaling molecules encoded not by nuclear DNA but by mitochondrial DNA. It was discovered in 2015 by Dr. Pinchas Cohen and his team at the USC Leonard Davis School of Gerontology.

MOTS-c is a 16-amino acid peptide: MRWQEMGYFY RKMSMGRSGG (single-letter code). It is encoded by the 12S ribosomal RNA gene in the mitochondrial genome — a highly conserved region, suggesting fundamental evolutionary importance.

MOTS-c circulates in the bloodstream and acts as a "mitochondrial hormone" — communicating mitochondrial status to the nucleus and to systemic tissues. It is considered one of the most important anti-aging molecular discoveries of the last decade.

Mechanism of Action (Molecular/Cellular Level)

AMPK Activation (Central Mechanism):

MOTS-c is a potent activator of AMP-activated protein kinase (AMPK) — the master metabolic regulator often called the "exercise enzyme" or "longevity switch." AMPK activation:

  • Increases glucose uptake and insulin sensitivity in muscle
  • Promotes fatty acid oxidation (fat burning)
  • Inhibits anabolic pathways that drive cellular senescence
  • Activates autophagy (cellular self-cleaning)
  • Inhibits mTORC1 (the aging/growth driver that must be modulated for longevity)

Folate-Methionine Cycle Inhibition → AMPK:

The mechanistic pathway: MOTS-c inhibits AICAR transformylase (an enzyme in purine synthesis that uses methylenetetrahydrofolate). This leads to accumulation of AICAR (5-Aminoimidazole-4-carboxamide ribonucleotide) — a direct AMPK activator. This is the unique molecular pathway linking mitochondrial status to nuclear AMPK signaling.

Nuclear Translocation & Gene Expression:

Under cellular stress (exercise, hypoxia, nutrient restriction), MOTS-c translocates from mitochondria to the nucleus, where it directly modulates gene expression — particularly stress response genes (ATF1, NRF2) and metabolic genes. This "mitochondria-to-nucleus retrograde signaling" is a newly discovered mechanism of cellular communication.

Insulin Sensitization:

MOTS-c dramatically improves insulin sensitivity in skeletal muscle — the primary site of glucose disposal. Mechanism: AMPK → GLUT4 translocation to cell membrane → enhanced glucose uptake without insulin signaling. This means it can improve metabolic function even in insulin-resistant states.

Anti-Inflammatory Action:

MOTS-c suppresses NF-κB-mediated inflammatory gene expression. In aging-related chronic inflammation ("inflammaging"), MOTS-c reduces circulating IL-6, TNF-α, and other SASP (senescence-associated secretory phenotype) factors.

Exercise Mimetic:

MOTS-c produces transcriptional profiles similar to aerobic exercise at the molecular level. It activates many of the same AMPK-dependent pathways as endurance exercise — earning it the description of an "exercise mimetic."

Age-Related Decline:

Critically, MOTS-c levels decline with age — and MOTS-c concentrations correlate with human longevity. In a landmark study, centenarians (100+ year lifespan) had significantly higher circulating MOTS-c levels than younger control groups.

MC1R is expressed predominantly on melanocytes (skin pigment cells). MT-I binds and activates MC1R with ~10x greater potency than native α-MSH due to its structural modifications.

MC1R activation → cAMP via adenylyl cyclase → PKA → MITF (Microphthalmia-Associated Transcription Factor) upregulation → transcription of melanogenesis enzymes:

  • Tyrosinase (rate-limiting enzyme)
  • TRP-1 and TRP-2 (downstream melanin synthesis)
  • → Eumelanin production (brown/black pigment — the UV-protective form)

UV Protection Mechanism:

Eumelanin produced by MT-I activation:

  • Absorbs UV-A, UV-B, and UV-C radiation
  • Acts as a radical scavenger (antioxidant), neutralizing reactive oxygen species generated by UV
  • Reduces UV-induced DNA damage (cyclobutane pyrimidine dimers — CPDs)
  • Reduces inflammatory cytokine release from UV-exposed keratinocytes (IL-1β, TNF-α)

DNA Repair Enhancement:

Beyond pigmentation, MC1R signaling directly enhances nucleotide excision repair (NER) — the primary mechanism for repairing UV-induced DNA damage. MC1R → p53 pathway modulation → enhanced repair enzyme recruitment at DNA damage sites.

Anti-Inflammatory Effects:

MT-I (via MC1R and other melanocortin receptors) inhibits NF-κB, suppresses inflammatory cytokines in skin and systemically, and modulates mast cell activation in the skin.

Selectivity Profile (key difference from Melanotan II):

  • MT-I is highly selective for MC1R

1. What Is VIP?

Vasoactive Intestinal Peptide (VIP) is a 28-amino acid neuropeptide naturally produced throughout the body — primarily in the hypothalamus, gut enteric nervous system, pancreas, lungs, immune cells, and peripheral neurons. It is one of the most pleiotropic peptides known: a single molecule that regulates inflammation, vasodilation, circadian rhythm, immune tolerance, gut motility, pulmonary function, and hormone secretion.

Despite its name, VIP's clinical relevance extends far beyond vascular effects. It is a master regulatory signal at the intersection of the nervous, immune, and endocrine systems.

Mechanism of Action (Molecular/Cellular Level)

VPAC1 & VPAC2 Receptor Binding: VIP exerts its effects through two G-protein-coupled receptors: VPAC1 (expressed broadly on immune cells, gut, brain) and VPAC2 (more selective: brain, smooth muscle, T-cells). Binding triggers adenylyl cyclase → cAMP → PKA signaling cascade with powerful downstream anti-inflammatory and regulatory effects.

Immune Modulation — Th1/Th2/Treg Balance:

  • Suppresses Th1 inflammatory cytokines: IL-2, IFN-γ, TNF-α, IL-6
  • Promotes Th2/regulatory responses: IL-4, IL-10, TGF-β
  • Directly induces regulatory T-cell (Treg) differentiation — the "immune tolerance" cells that prevent autoimmune overactivation
  • Potently suppresses macrophage activation and dendritic cell maturation
  • This makes VIP a major player in autoimmune conditions, mast cell disorders, and MCAS

Vasodilation: VIP relaxes vascular smooth muscle through cAMP-mediated pathway, independent of NO. Reduces pulmonary vascular resistance particularly effectively — clinical applications in pulmonary hypertension.

Circadian Rhythm Regulation: VIP is the primary neurotransmitter of the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) — the brain's master clock. VIP neurons synchronize the circadian pacemaker, coordinating sleep-wake cycles, hormone release timing, and metabolic rhythms. VIP deficiency → circadian dysfunction, poor sleep architecture, hormone dysregulation.

Gut Function: Relaxes GI smooth muscle, regulates intestinal secretion, modulates ENS (enteric nervous system) signaling. Anti-inflammatory in the gut — reduces intestinal inflammation via suppression of mucosal immune activation.

Neuroprotection: VIP promotes neuronal survival, inhibits apoptosis in oxidative/excitotoxic conditions, and stimulates BDNF and activity-dependent neuroprotective protein (ADNP) secretion from astrocytes.

Pulmonary: Bronchodilatory, reduces airway inflammation, modulates mast cell degranulation in the lungs. Historically investigated as a treatment for pulmonary arterial hypertension (PAH).

CIPA (Cytokine-Induced Pulmonary Inflammation): VIP is the most potent endogenous suppressor of cytokine storm in lung tissue — directly relevant to COVID-related pathology.

1. What Is FST-344?

Follistatin-344 (FST-344) is one of three isoforms of follistatin — a naturally occurring glycoprotein that functions as a potent endogenous inhibitor of activin, myostatin, and other TGF-β superfamily members. FST-344 is the predominant form found in circulating plasma and is produced primarily in the liver, ovaries, and pituitary gland.

The number "344" refers to the amino acid length — 344 amino acids — which corresponds to the most biologically active circulating isoform. The other major isoforms are FST-288 (tissue-bound, gonadal dominant) and FST-315 (also circulating).

FST-344 became the subject of intense research and popular attention when studies in animals with myostatin inhibition (genetic knockouts or follistatin overexpression) showed extraordinary muscle hypertrophy — the "mighty mouse" and "double-muscled" cattle phenomena are related to myostatin deficiency.

Mechanism of Action (Molecular/Cellular Level)

Myostatin (GDF-8) Inhibition — Primary Mechanism:

Myostatin is the body's primary "muscle brake" — a TGF-β superfamily member that limits muscle growth by:

  • Inhibiting satellite cell (myogenic progenitor) activation
  • Suppressing myoblast proliferation and differentiation
  • Reducing protein synthesis signaling
  • Promoting muscle protein catabolism

FST-344 binds myostatin with extremely high affinity (Kd ~1 nM), forming a stable, inert complex that neutralizes myostatin activity. Without myostatin brake:

  • Satellite cells are released from inhibition → proliferate → fuse with muscle fibers
  • Protein synthesis signaling (mTOR, Akt) operates at higher capacity
  • Muscle hypertrophy and hyperplasia can proceed
  • Net: muscle grows beyond its "natural ceiling"

Activin A/B Inhibition:

FST-344 also potently inhibits activin A and B (other TGF-β members):

  • In muscle: activin A promotes catabolism and inhibits growth (similar to myostatin); blocking it amplifies the anabolic effect
  • In reproductive tissue: activin regulates FSH; FST inhibition has hormone-relevant effects
  • In bone: activin inhibition promotes bone formation
  • Anti-fibrotic: activin A is a key driver of fibrosis; FST-344's inhibition of activin A reduces fibrotic scar formation in muscle and other tissues

BMP (Bone Morphogenic Protein) Modulation:

FST-344 also binds select BMPs (GDF-11, BMP-2, BMP-4, BMP-7):

  • Modulation of BMPs affects bone formation, cardiac muscle, and neural development
  • GDF-11 binding is particularly relevant: GDF-11 is debated as an "aging factor" in circulation; FST-344's inhibition of GDF-11 may have anti-aging implications

Anti-Fibrotic Action:

Beyond muscle, FST-344's inhibition of TGF-β family members (particularly activin A and GDF-8) reduces pathological fibrosis in:

  • Muscle (replacing fibrosis with functional tissue)
  • Liver (anti-fibrotic in NASH models)
  • Lungs (anti-fibrotic in pulmonary fibrosis models)
  • Heart (anti-fibrotic post-MI)

While α-MSH is best known for melanin production (through MT-I/II), its C-terminal fragment KPV has potent and distinct anti-inflammatory and gut-protective properties with virtually none of the melanogenic or sexual effects of full α-MSH.

KPV Mechanism of Action

NF-κB Inhibition:

KPV directly inhibits NF-κB nuclear translocation in macrophages, dendritic cells, and epithelial cells — reducing expression of the entire inflammatory gene program: IL-1β, TNF-α, IL-6, IL-12, COX-2. This is the same master inflammatory pathway that NSAIDs and corticosteroids act on, but through a fundamentally different receptor mechanism.

Melanocortin Receptor Binding (MC1R, MC3R):

KPV binds MC1R and MC3R — but NOT MC4R (no sexual/libido effects) and NOT MC2R (no adrenal/cortisol effects). MC1R and MC3R activation triggers the downstream anti-inflammatory cAMP cascade, delivering potent resolution of inflammation.

Gut Mucosal Protection:

KPV has direct cytoprotective effects on intestinal epithelial cells:

  • Reduces intestinal permeability (preserves tight junction integrity)
  • Suppresses mucosal NF-κB — reducing local inflammatory mediators in the gut lumen
  • Reduces mast cell activation in the gut wall
  • Particularly relevant in IBD: KPV suppresses colitis pathology in animal models as effectively as several IBD drugs

Intracellular Delivery:

Remarkably, KPV is transported intracellularly via the PepT1 (peptide transporter 1) system — which is upregulated in inflamed intestinal epithelium. This creates a "targeted delivery" effect: the more inflamed the gut, the more KPV is taken up into inflamed cells. This is why oral KPV is particularly effective for gut inflammation despite being a peptide.

Wound Healing Promotion:

Beyond inflammation, KPV promotes keratinocyte migration and wound closure — complementing the other components of KLOW.

  • Results: KPV treatment significantly reduced colon shortening, mucosal damage score, and inflammatory cell infiltration. Histological healing comparable to mesalazine (a standard IBD drug). NF-κB activity in colon tissue reduced 65%.

9. FAQ — For Rep Use

Q: Who should get KLOW vs. GLOW?

A: KLOW is the premium choice for clients who have: (1) gut inflammation alongside physical injury, (2) IBD/Crohn's/colitis, (3) MCAS or systemic inflammatory conditions, (4) autoimmune conditions where NF-κB suppression is especially important, or (5) clients on NSAIDs who need gut protection. GLOW is excellent for straightforward musculoskeletal recovery without the gut/autoimmune component.

Q: What does KPV add that BPC-157 doesn't already provide?

A: BPC-157 is pro-healing with anti-inflammatory properties (via NO/COX-2 pathway). KPV adds a completely different anti-inflammatory mechanism: MC1R/MC3R → NF-κB suppression → direct cytokine storm prevention. BPC-157 heals the tissue; KPV quiets the immune overactivation. For clients with IBD, MCAS, or significant systemic inflammation, KPV is the critical differentiator.

Q: Can KLOW be taken orally?

A: The BPC-157 and KPV components have documented oral activity. The full KLOW stack oral delivery isn't standardized, but oral forms of the BPC-157 and KPV components are used in IBD protocols. For systemic musculoskeletal use, SubQ injection is recommended.

Q: Is the GHK-Cu dose in KLOW very high?

A: At 50mg, the GHK-Cu component dominates the formulation by weight. This is intentional — GHK-Cu drives the remodeling phase of healing (collagen quality, anti-fibrotic, anti-aging gene expression) and is safe at high doses. The high copper peptide loading ensures the remodeling phase is thoroughly supported.

Q: How does KLOW compare to PRP or stem cell injections?

A: PRP and stem cells are regenerative treatments requiring clinical procedures and specialized equipment. KLOW is a peptide protocol clients can administer SubQ at home after training. They're not mutually exclusive — many clinicians pair KLOW with PRP/prolotherapy for synergistic effects. KLOW's advantage: systemic reach, daily dosing convenience, comprehensive multi-mechanism action.
