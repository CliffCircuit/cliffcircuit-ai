Electricians Quote Big Jobs Then Disappear

March 6, 2026 · 5 min read

Residential electricians land appointments for panel upgrades, EV charger installs, and whole-home rewires — jobs worth $3,000 to $15,000 — send a quote, and then go quiet. The homeowner collects three bids and books whoever called them back. I mapped where those large-job estimates actually go. It's almost never a better price.

The Quote That Nobody Followed Up On

A homeowner wants a panel upgrade. Maybe they're adding an EV charger, maybe they're finishing a basement, maybe the inspector flagged it on a sale. They call three electricians. All three show up, assess the job, and send quotes within a week.

Then nothing happens. All three electricians go back to the active jobs on their schedule. The homeowner has three proposals in their inbox and a decision to make, and nobody calls them back.

Two weeks later, one electrician texts with a brief follow-up. The homeowner books them. Not because they were cheapest. Not because their proposal was clearest. Because they showed up again when everyone else had moved on.

I mapped this pattern specifically for residential electrical contractors who do large-scope work: panel replacements, EV charger installs, whole-home rewires. The jobs worth $3,000 to $15,000 that should be won but often aren't. Here's where they actually go.

Why Large-Job Follow-Up Is Different

Small service calls — a tripped breaker, a dead outlet — close fast. The homeowner has a problem right now, they book whoever answers. The urgency is built in.

Large projects are different. The homeowner has multiple quotes, needs to think it through, might be timing it around other renovations or financing. The decision window is longer. And in that longer window, the electrician who stays present — without being annoying — wins the job.

Most electricians treat large-job quotes the same way they treat small service work: quote it and wait. That works fine when there's urgency. For a $9,000 panel upgrade where the homeowner has three weeks to decide? Waiting is how you lose.

The Mistake I Made on Day 4

The first version of the Day 4 message in the follow-up sequence was price-focused. Something like: "We're flexible on the quote if pricing is a concern — let us know what you're working with."

A contractor who runs a quality electrical shop in the Pacific Northwest told me exactly why that was wrong: "Opening with price flexibility signals weakness. Now they think the original quote was inflated. And once they think that, they're going to negotiate everything else too."

He was right. For contractors who position at the quality end of the market, leading with price concessions undermines the thing that makes them worth the premium. The fix was to lead with scheduling context instead of pricing.

The revised Day 4 message: "Checking in on the panel upgrade quote — we tend to book out 2-3 weeks for large projects, so wanted to flag timing if you're hoping to complete it before [season or milestone]." Useful, not pushy, and doesn't invite a price conversation the contractor didn't want to have.

The 3-Message Sequence

For large-job residential electrical work, three messages cover the decision window without becoming noise.

Day 4 — Scheduling context: As described above. This is the first reach-back after sending the quote. Brief, useful, not a nudge.

Day 9 — Question invitation: "Happy to walk through the scope or materials if anything in the proposal was unclear — large projects like this are worth getting the details right." This hits right around when homeowners are typically narrowing from three quotes to two. An offer to clarify the proposal is genuinely useful at this stage, and it creates a natural reason for the homeowner to re-engage.

Day 14 — Soft close: "Following up on the quote we sent — we have some availability in the next few weeks and wanted to check if you'd decided on timing." Clean, direct, not desperate. This is the last message in the sequence. If no response, the lead goes dormant and into an annual reactivation pool.

Change Order Text Confirmation

One of the biggest sources of invoice disputes in electrical work isn't the original scope — it's the changes that happen mid-project. The electrician discovers the panel location needs to shift. The permit requires an additional circuit. Materials substitution is needed because the original spec isn't available.

Most electricians handle this verbally. They tell the homeowner, the homeowner says fine, and three weeks later when the final invoice is 15% higher, the homeowner doesn't remember authorizing the changes.

Text confirmation closes this gap completely. Whenever a change to scope or materials comes up, a brief text: "Quick update — [specific change] is going to add [amount] to the project. Just want to make sure that's okay before we proceed. Reply to confirm." The homeowner confirms in writing. The invoice matches what they agreed to. No surprises, no disputes.

For the electricians I've worked with who implement this, invoice dispute rates drop significantly. Not because the homeowners are unhappy with the changes — most of them are fine with necessary modifications. The disputes come from not remembering the conversation. Written confirmation prevents that.

The Referral Request That Most Contractors Skip

Electrical work generates word-of-mouth referrals disproportionately well, because it's a service most homeowners hire infrequently and care about getting right. A homeowner who has a great experience with a panel upgrade will tell every neighbor who mentions electrical work for years. If the contractor asks.

Most don't. They finish the job, the homeowner is satisfied, and the contractor moves on to the next project. The referral conversation never happens.

The post-job referral sequence is simple: five days after job completion, a brief message. "Hope everything is working well — panel upgrades tend to be invisible once they're done, which is exactly how they should be. If you have neighbors or friends who are thinking about EV chargers or electrical upgrades, we'd appreciate the referral. Happy to send them something."

Timing matters here. Right at five days, the homeowner still has the positive feeling from the completed project and hasn't yet moved on entirely. Earlier feels rushed. Later, they've moved on.

The electrician I worked with most closely on this tracked referral sources for six months after implementing the sequence. The post-job referral ask generated more inbound than his Google listing for three of those six months. Word of mouth in trades is powerful. Asking for it is the part that most contractors forget.

What I Actually Learned

Electricians lose large jobs they should win for one reason: the quote goes out, and then nothing happens until the homeowner is so far into their decision process that the window has closed.

The fix isn't a new sales approach. It's just following up — three times, usefully, in a way that makes the homeowner feel served rather than sold. The contractor who does that wins a meaningful percentage of the bids where they were otherwise equivalent to the competition on price and quality.

The change order and referral layers are the business development pieces on top of that — the difference between a contractor who does great work and one who builds a practice that sustains itself from repeat and referred business. Both are worth building. The sequence is just the starting point.
