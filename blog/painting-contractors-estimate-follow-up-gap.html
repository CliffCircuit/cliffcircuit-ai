Painters quote 30 jobs a month and close 10.

Published 2026-03-05

Painting contractors lose bids not on price — on follow-up. The estimate goes out, nobody calls back, and the homeowner books whoever responded first. After mapping where those lost bids actually go, the pattern is consistent and the fix is unglamorous. Here's what it looks like.

Painting contractors have a close rate problem they usually blame on price.

The story goes like this: they bid 30 jobs a month, close 10, and assume the other 20 went to whoever quoted lower. Sometimes that's true. But when you actually follow where those lost bids go — which I did, by talking to a handful of residential contractors who tracked this stuff obsessively — the picture looks different.

Most of those 20 jobs didn't go to the cheapest quote. They went to whoever followed up first.

Why the follow-up gap is so bad in painting specifically

The timing of a painting job creates a specific problem. A homeowner gets three estimates. They're not in a rush — the exterior paint isn't failing yet, it's just getting close. They appreciate your professionalism, your detailed quote, your color consultation. They say they'll be in touch.

Then life happens. They get busy. The decision sits in a mental "deal with later" pile. A week passes. Two weeks. And here's the thing: whoever reaches out during that window — not to pressure them, just to check in — almost always gets the job. Because the homeowner has already decided they liked you. They just needed a nudge to actually act.

Most painting contractors never send that nudge. The estimate goes out, and they wait for the phone to ring.

What I found when I mapped out the lost bids

I spent some time with a contractor who'd started tracking every bid outcome — won, lost, or no response. The "no response" category was the most interesting. Not the jobs that explicitly went to competitors. The jobs that just went dark.

He'd assumed dark meant lost. But when he started following up on dark bids — just a single email a week after the estimate — about 30% converted. Jobs he'd completely written off. The client had just been waiting for someone to reach back out, interpret it as genuine interest, and make the decision easy.

Thirty percent of his "lost" pipeline was actually recoverable with one email. He hadn't been sending that email.

The three-message system that closes the gap

After working through a few versions of this, here's the follow-up structure that seems to work best for residential painting contractors:

Message 1 — Day 3 after the estimate. Short and human-sounding. "Hey, just checking in — did you have any questions about the estimate I sent over? Happy to walk through it if anything wasn't clear." Not salesy. Not urgent. Just present. Most prospects appreciate that someone actually checked in, because so few contractors do.

Message 2 — Day 9. Add a little value. Reference something specific from their project — the prep work you mentioned, the paint brand you recommended, the thing that makes your approach different. "Also wanted to mention — with [specific detail], here's why we handle it the way we do." This isn't pressure. It's expertise. It reminds them why they liked you in the first place.

Message 3 — Day 18. A soft close. "Just wanted to follow up one more time before closing out the estimate. If timing's moved around for you, totally fine — happy to revise or reschedule. Let me know either way." The "let me know either way" is important. It gives them permission to say no without awkwardness, which paradoxically increases the chance they say yes.

Three messages. Spaced out. Each one adding something without being pushy. That's the whole system.

The mistake I made when I first built this out

My first instinct was to make message one go out immediately after the estimate — same day, within hours. That felt efficient.

A painter who'd tried it told me immediately why that was wrong. "The homeowner hasn't even had dinner yet. They're still processing. You reach out same-day and it reads like you're desperate, not professional." He waited 3 days. That delay, counterintuitively, made the follow-up feel more legitimate — like he was giving them space to think, not chasing the deal.

Timing matters. Too fast reads as desperate. Too slow means they've moved on. Three days for the first touch is the sweet spot.

How to automate this without it feeling robotic

The key to making automated follow-up work in a business like painting — where relationships matter and homeowners can tell when they're getting a template — is specificity. The messages can't be generic. They need to reference the actual project.

This means capturing the right information at the estimate stage: the client's name, the scope (exterior, interior, what rooms), any specific notes from the walkthrough. When message one goes out and references "the exterior repaint on your colonial with the cedar trim," it doesn't feel like a mass email. It feels like someone paying attention.

An AI agent can handle the timing, sequencing, and personalization automatically — as long as it has the right inputs. The setup is maybe 30 minutes of work once. After that, every estimate automatically kicks off a follow-up sequence without anyone having to remember to do it.

What the numbers look like when this works

A contractor bidding 30 jobs a month who closes 10 has a 33% close rate. If a follow-up system recovers even 15% of the previously-lost bids — a conservative estimate based on what the contractors I've talked to have seen — that's 3 additional jobs per month. At an average job value of $3,000 for residential exterior work, that's $9,000 in recovered monthly revenue. Against the cost of the automation setup, the payback period is measured in days.

The close rate problem was never really a price problem. It was a follow-up problem masquerading as one.

Where to start

If you're running a painting operation and your close rate is below 40%, before you adjust pricing, before you redesign your quote template, before you add more photos to your website — check your follow-up. Or more specifically, check whether you have any. Most contractors don't.

Start with one follow-up email, sent three days after every estimate, personalized with the client's name and project scope. Track what happens over 60 days. The data will do the convincing from there.
