HVAC service agreements expire in silence. An AI agent fixes that.

HVAC companies have one of the best recurring revenue models in the trades — service agreements, predictable margin, high retention. But they expire silently at a 40% rate, not because customers are unhappy, but because nobody asked at the right moment. Here's what a follow-up agent actually changes.

An HVAC company fixes your furnace in January. You call at 9pm, they have someone there by 7am. You're grateful, you leave a five-star review, and you mean it. They're slammed — six more emergency calls that week, installs backed up until March.

Six months later your AC needs a spring tune-up. Who do you call? Probably whoever comes up first on Google. Maybe whoever left a flyer on your door. Almost certainly not the company that fixed your furnace — because they never reached out.

This is the service agreement problem. It sounds like a customer loyalty problem, but it's actually a follow-up problem. HVAC companies have one of the best recurring revenue models in the trades: annual service agreements, two visits per year (heating check in fall, cooling check in spring), predictable margin, high retention. On paper, it's a great business. In practice, those agreements expire silently at a rate that would genuinely shock most owners if they pulled the numbers.

What the data actually looks like

I looked into this after helping a small HVAC shop outside Sacramento figure out why their service revenue was flat despite growing their customer base. They had 340 active service agreement customers at the start of the year. By renewal season, 140 had lapsed. That's a 41% attrition rate on their most reliable revenue stream.

When I asked the owner how many of those 140 customers left because they were unhappy, he thought for a second and said: "Probably five. Maybe ten. The rest just... didn't hear from us."

That's the honest answer. Customers don't cancel. They drift. They forget the agreement existed. They assume it auto-renewed and discover it didn't when something breaks. Or they get a postcard from a competitor at exactly the wrong moment — right when their agreement is about to lapse — and book with them instead.

A 40% annual churn rate on a service agreement portfolio isn't unusual. Neither is a 50% rate for smaller shops without a dedicated admin function. Most owners I've talked to assume their churn is around 15-20% because that's what active cancellations look like. The silent drift is invisible until you count it.

What silence is actually costing you

Do the math on a 200-customer service base at $200/year per agreement. That's $40,000 in recurring revenue annually. A 40% drift rate means $16,000 walks out the door every year from customers who had zero complaints and would probably have renewed if anyone asked.

That's before counting the downstream losses. Every lapsed service agreement customer is someone who'll call a competitor for their next repair. The tune-up you didn't do because the agreement lapsed is the tune-up someone else did — and that other company is now the one who gets called when the system fails at midnight in August.

Service agreements aren't just recurring revenue. They're the relationship that turns a one-time emergency customer into a 15-year account. When they expire silently, you lose both the renewal and the future.

Why follow-up doesn't happen

This one's straightforward. HVAC shops are run by technicians who got good enough at their trade to start a company. The owner is usually in the field or managing installs. Admin is handling incoming calls, scheduling, and dispatch. Nobody has a dedicated person whose job is to work a renewal list every month.

In theory, most CRMs can generate a list of expiring agreements. In practice, that list sits in a tab that nobody has time to touch because today's installs and tomorrow's emergency slots are more immediately pressing.

The follow-up that gets skipped on a Tuesday becomes the customer that drifted by March. Multiply that by a year and you've got your 40% churn rate.

This is exactly the kind of task that looks like a people problem but is actually a systems problem. The intent is there — nobody wants to lose a customer — but there's no reliable mechanism to convert intent into action at the right time, consistently, for every single expiring agreement.

What an AI agent actually does here

I built out a version of this for the Sacramento shop. Here's how it works:

The AI connects to their service agreement database (in their case, ServiceTitan, but this works with most field service CRMs). It monitors expiration dates continuously. At 30 days out, it triggers a renewal sequence.

Day 1: A personalized text goes out. First name, reference to the last service date, a line about what's coming up seasonally. Not a generic blast. Something that sounds like a person who knows their account wrote it.

Day 5, if no response: One follow-up. Different phrasing. Still brief. No desperation.

After that: The AI logs the non-response, flags the account for the service manager to decide whether to make a personal call, and moves on. Two touches total. It does not continue badgering people.

For customers who respond, it routes them either to an online booking link or to a phone call with dispatch, depending on what they indicate. It doesn't try to close the agreement itself — it just gets the right people talking.

High-value accounts (5+ years, premium system, commercial) get flagged separately for the owner or service manager to call personally. The AI handles the volume; humans handle the relationship accounts.

The thing I got wrong the first time

The first version of this system I helped build — for a pool service company running a similar recurring revenue model — had three follow-up touches instead of two. The logic seemed sound: more attempts, more conversions.

The response from customers was not what we expected. Several complained that it felt pushy. One told the owner she felt like she was being harassed by a robot. The owner called me slightly annoyed.

We cut it back to two touches, rewrote the second message to be softer, and the complaint rate dropped to zero. Conversion rate stayed roughly the same. Turns out the third message wasn't winning renewals — it was just annoying people who'd already decided.

Two is the ceiling. One reminder, one gentle follow-up. Log the non-response and let a human decide if it's worth a personal call. Beyond that, you're burning goodwill for no gain.

What a real week looks like

Here's an actual week from the Sacramento shop, about three months after we got the system running:

Monday: System flags 14 agreements expiring in the next 30 days. Texts scheduled automatically for Tuesday morning.

Tuesday: 14 personalized texts sent. By end of day, 6 customers have responded. 4 booked immediately via the link. 2 asked questions that dispatch handled by phone. 8 no responses.

Thursday: Follow-up texts sent to the 8 non-responders. 3 book over the weekend. 2 call dispatch. 3 don't respond.

End of week: 9 renewals from 14 expiring agreements — a 64% save rate. The 5 non-responders get logged and reviewed by the service manager on Friday. He calls 2 of them personally (both long-tenure customers). Gets 1 additional renewal. The other 4 lapse.

Net result: 10 renewals out of 14 potential lapses. Before the system, the historical rate was maybe 3 or 4 out of 14 — whoever happened to call in to ask about something else and got reminded by a sharp dispatcher.

That difference, across a full year, is the difference between a 20% attrition rate and a 40% one.

One thing this doesn't fix

The AI handles follow-up volume at scale. It doesn't fix agreements that were underpriced, or customers who had a bad service experience and are looking for a reason to leave. If your service quality has issues, an automated renewal sequence will accelerate churn by giving dissatisfied customers a natural exit point.

The shop in Sacramento had a two-star review problem that had nothing to do with their follow-up process. The AI would have flagged those customers for renewal outreach right alongside the happy ones. We built in a filter: any customer with an open complaint or a negative review from the past 12 months gets routed to the owner's personal call list, not the automated sequence.

Worth thinking about before you automate anything — garbage in, garbage out still applies. If there's a reason customers are drifting beyond just not hearing from you, fix that first.

What to do with this

If you run an HVAC shop and you haven't pulled your service agreement attrition numbers recently, start there. Export your agreements from 12 months ago and count how many are still active. The number is probably worse than you think.

If the attrition is high and the cause is follow-up gaps rather than service quality, this is a solvable problem. The technology isn't complicated — it's a connection between your CRM data and a message-sending system with some logic about timing and content. The hard part is building the discipline to actually set it up and trust it to run.

For most small HVAC shops, that discipline comes from seeing the first week of results and realizing you were leaving 60-70% of expiring agreements on the table by default.

The agreements don't expire because customers don't want to renew. They expire because nobody asked at the right moment. An AI agent is just a system that asks, every time, without forgetting.
