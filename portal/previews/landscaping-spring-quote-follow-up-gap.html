Landscapers send 200 spring quotes. Most follow up on zero. An AI agent fixes that.

Draft

Every March, landscaping companies blast out hundreds of estimates for seasonal cleanups, installs, and maintenance contracts — then go silent. The quote sits in someone's inbox for two weeks until they hire the other guy. I dug into why this happens and what an AI follow-up agent actually looks like when it works.

March hits and every landscaping company in the country does the same thing: they quote everything in sight. Cleanup jobs, new installs, irrigation startups, seasonal maintenance contracts. The phones are ringing, the estimators are busy, and by the end of the week there are 40 open quotes floating out there.

Then they go install a paver patio. And another one. And suddenly it's the second week of April and those quotes are stone cold.

I've been thinking about this particular gap for a while because it's so avoidable — and so common. Landscaping is the rare industry where the sales season and the installation season collide at exactly the same time. You're trying to close jobs while simultaneously doing them. Something gets dropped. It's almost always the follow-up.

The quote graveyard problem

Here's the math that stings: the average landscaping company closes about 30% of its quotes. Which sounds reasonable until you ask how many of those customers just... decided on their own, without anyone following up. A lot of them. Industry surveys consistently show that most buying decisions in home services happen after the second or third touchpoint — but most landscaping companies make zero touchpoints after the initial estimate.

You send a quote. You get busy. Two weeks pass. The homeowner hired the guy who texted them on day three.

It's not that the homeowner didn't want your service. It's that the other guy showed up (digitally) when you didn't. Attention is a currency in this business, and in spring, most landscapers are totally broke in that department.

Why landscapers don't follow up (it's not laziness)

I want to be honest about this because the easy story is "landscapers are disorganized." That's not what's actually happening.

Landscaping owners are often solo or two-person operations running a crew of four to eight. The owner quotes in the morning, manages jobs in the afternoon, and orders materials at night. There is no sales team. There is no CRM with automated reminders. There's a spreadsheet, maybe, and a stack of printed estimates in the truck.

When I dug into this with a few owners last fall, one of them told me something that stuck: "I want to follow up. I just forget who I haven't heard back from. By the time I remember, I feel weird calling because it's been two weeks."

That's the real problem. It's not laziness — it's the gap between intent and execution, multiplied by a job schedule that leaves no margin. When every hour of your day is spoken for by actual work, the follow-up that might win you next week's revenue gets crushed by this week's.

What an AI follow-up agent actually does here

This is the specific use case I find interesting: not a chatbot, not a voice assistant, but a lightweight AI agent that watches for open quotes and sends follow-up messages at the right intervals.

The setup I'd build for a landscaping company looks like this:

When a new estimate goes out — whether it's emailed from their quoting software or texted manually — the agent logs it with a timestamp. Three days later, if no response has come in, it sends a short, warm text from the owner's number. Something like: "Hey, just checking in on the estimate I sent over for the spring cleanup. Any questions before you decide?" That's it. No sales pressure, no discount bait. Just a human-feeling nudge.

If there's still no response after another four days, it sends one more. Different wording. Then it stops. Two touchpoints, automatically, without the owner thinking about it at all.

When a customer responds — yes, no, or "can we adjust the scope?" — the agent flags it for the owner to handle personally. The AI handles the routine follow-up. The human handles the actual conversation.

The failure I didn't anticipate

When I first thought through this flow, I assumed the hard part would be the technical integration — getting the quoting software to talk to the messaging layer. That's actually the easy part. Most quoting tools have webhooks or at least exportable CSVs. You can build the trigger in a few hours.

The hard part was tone calibration. The first version of the follow-up messages I drafted sounded exactly like what they were: automated. "This is a follow-up regarding your estimate #4821 dated March 12th." Terrible. No one responds to that.

The second problem was that some customers had already said no — verbally, in a conversation with the owner that never made it into any system. So the agent was following up on a quote the customer had already declined in person. Awkward.

The fix for both problems: a simple "status update" step the owner completes each morning. Takes 90 seconds. They scan the open quote list and mark anything that should be closed. The agent only follows up on quotes the owner hasn't touched. It's not fully automated, but it's close — and more importantly, it doesn't embarrass anyone.

The spring maintenance contract angle

The real money for landscapers isn't one-time installs — it's recurring maintenance contracts. Weekly mowing, seasonal fertilization, fall cleanup, the whole program. These contracts are worth $2,000 to $6,000 per customer per year, and landscapers lose them constantly to competitors who are slightly more persistent.

An AI agent built for contract renewal follow-up works slightly differently. Instead of watching for open quotes, it watches the customer list for anyone who had a maintenance contract last year but hasn't signed one this year. In early March, it sends a personalized note: "Coming up on spring — want to get you back on the schedule before slots fill up."

That framing — scarcity combined with relationship continuity — closes at a meaningfully higher rate than a generic "ready for spring?" blast. And because it's personalized to each customer's history, it doesn't feel like mass marketing. It feels like the owner remembered them.

Which is the point. The AI doesn't replace the relationship. It extends the owner's capacity to maintain it.

What this costs to set up (realistic numbers)

I want to give actual numbers here rather than vague gestures at "AI tools." A basic version of this — quote follow-up and contract renewal reminders — can be built in OpenClaw in a weekend. The agent logic itself takes maybe four hours if you know what you're doing. Integration with common quoting tools (Jobber, Housecall Pro, even QuickBooks) adds another few hours depending on how clean their export is.

Ongoing cost is minimal. The AI calls for basic follow-up texts are fractions of a penny each. Even if you're sending 500 follow-up messages a month, you're looking at under $5 in API costs.

The harder cost is the 90-second daily status check I mentioned. That's a habit change, and habit changes are genuinely difficult for busy owners. If that friction kills adoption, nothing else matters. The best version of this system has a friction floor that's low enough to actually use.

The close rate math

If a landscaping company sends 200 quotes in spring with a 30% close rate, they're closing 60 jobs. What if systematic follow-up moved that to 38%? That's 76 jobs — 16 extra jobs from the same marketing and estimating work they were already doing.

At an average job value of $1,200, that's $19,200 in additional revenue. From texting.

I'm not promising those exact numbers — the right increase depends on why quotes were dying in the first place. But the directional math is sound: the quotes are already paid for. The labor of estimating is already done. Recovering even a portion of the deals you're leaving silent costs almost nothing.

What I'd actually build

If I were setting this up for a landscaping company today, the stack would be: Jobber for quoting and job management (most landscapers already use it), OpenClaw as the AI layer with a simple cron agent that checks quote status each morning, and Twilio for the actual text sends. The owner gets a daily "here's what's going out today" summary so nothing is a surprise.

Phase two — once that's running cleanly — would be the contract renewal flow I described above. That's higher value and slightly more complex, but once the infrastructure is there, it's mostly logic work.

Phase three, if there's appetite for it, would be a post-job follow-up sequence that asks for reviews and referrals automatically. Same infrastructure, different trigger. But that's getting ahead of things.

The real question

Here's what I keep coming back to: landscaping owners are not the problem. The industry has a structural mismatch between when business is won and when it's available to be won. Spring is simultaneously your busiest delivery season and your most important sales season. You can't solve that by working harder.

You solve it by offloading the parts that don't require you — the follow-up texts, the renewal nudges, the check-ins — to something that doesn't get tired and doesn't forget.

The quote you sent three days ago is still sitting in someone's inbox right now. Are you going to follow up on it — or are you going to find out in April that they hired the other guy?
