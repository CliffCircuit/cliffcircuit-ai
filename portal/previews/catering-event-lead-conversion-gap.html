Catering: Cold Leads Between Tasting and Decision

Event catering has one of the longest sales cycles in any service business — weeks between the first inquiry, the tasting, and the signed contract. Most catering companies treat that window as the client's decision process. It is. But it's also where a competitor who follows up consistently quietly takes the booking. I mapped where catering leads actually go cold.

The Long Sales Cycle Problem

Event catering has the longest sales cycle in the service business world. A wedding inquiry might come in 14 months before the event. A corporate gala books 6-8 months out. Even smaller private events — birthday dinners, rehearsal dinners, holiday parties — typically run 4-8 weeks from first inquiry to signed contract.

In that window, the catering company that wins the booking is almost never the cheapest. It's the one that stayed present in the most useful way throughout the decision process.

I mapped where catering leads actually go cold, and what a properly timed follow-up sequence does to close rates. The pattern is consistent across event types, even though the timelines are different.

Where Leads Actually Go Cold

Catering inquiries come in and get a response — most companies handle this part reasonably well. An initial call or email exchange establishes the event details, and a tasting or proposal meeting gets scheduled.

The drop-off happens after the tasting. The client has tasted the food, gotten a proposal, and now enters the decision phase. They're comparing your proposal to one or two others. They're debating budget. They're talking to the venue about what other caterers they recommend. They're deciding whether to use the preferred caterer on the venue's list or go with someone they found independently.

Most catering companies check in once during this window and then wait. The client is left to make their decision in a vacuum, with the only information they have being what they gathered during the tasting.

The caterer who wins is usually the one who showed up — helpfully, not pushily — two or three more times during that window. Not with pressure. With useful information and genuine service offers.

The Scarcity Tactic That Backfired

The first follow-up sequence I built for a catering company included a Day 10 message that opened with availability pressure: "Your event date is coming up on our calendar — wanted to check in before we finalized commitments."

A wedding planner I showed this to flagged it immediately. "Every caterer sends some version of this. Nobody believes it. It just makes you sound desperate."

She was right. "Your date is filling up" is one of the most used and least credible tactics in events industry sales. Clients know it. It makes them trust you less, not more.

The fix was to bury the availability context inside a genuine service offer. Instead of: "Your date is at risk." Something like: "A lot of clients find it helpful to do a soft-hold on a date while they finalize details — if that would be useful, I can note your date as pending in our calendar, no commitment required." Practical, useful, not pressured. And it accomplishes the same scheduling purpose without the manipulation signal.

The Follow-Up Sequence by Event Type

The timing varies by event type because the decision windows are different, but the structure is consistent.

For weddings (long cycle, high stakes): After the tasting, three touchpoints in the following three to four weeks. First: a personal note from the catering director with a specific detail from the tasting ("The salmon was a standout — we'd love to feature it if you decide to go in that direction"). Second, week 2: a useful resource offer ("Happy to provide a proposed timeline for the reception day if it would help your planning — no commitment, just a useful document"). Third, week 3-4: the soft-hold offer as described above.

For corporate events (mid cycle, budget-driven): The decision window is shorter and the stakes feel lower, which means clients move faster but also cut contact faster. Two follow-ups in the first 10 days. First: proposal clarification offer ("Happy to adjust scope or pricing tiers if the initial proposal doesn't quite fit the budget"). Second: a question about timeline and decision process ("Are there other stakeholders who need to be involved in the decision? Happy to prepare any materials that would help the conversation internally").

For private events (short cycle, relationship-driven): The client often knows you by reputation. The follow-up is warmer and more personal. After the initial inquiry, a brief check-in around day 5: "Had a few ideas after we spoke about what would work well for your dinner format — want to hop on a quick call to share them?" Creates momentum without a proposal-review formality.

The Vendor Network Layer

Catering is referral-heavy in a specific way. Venues, wedding planners, event coordinators — these relationships drive a disproportionate share of new bookings. A catering company that's on a venue's preferred vendor list gets access to every bride who tours that venue. A wedding planner who loves working with a particular caterer recommends them consistently.

Most catering companies maintain these relationships informally. They do good work, the planner remembers them for the next booking, repeat. That works until the planner changes venues, the venue hires a new coordinator, or a competitor starts actively cultivating the relationship.

The vendor relationship sequence runs on a separate track from client follow-up. Quarterly check-in with active referral partners. Brief, genuinely useful: "We have an opening in June that would work well for an intimate dinner if any of your clients are looking — wanted to flag it." Specific, not generic. It keeps the caterer top of mind without feeling like a mass email.

The companies I've seen do this well treat their vendor network as a separate CRM track. The contact cadence is lighter than client follow-up but consistent. The content is specific to each relationship. And the maintenance is what keeps referrals flowing in years 3 and 5, not just in the first flush of a new vendor relationship.

The Post-Event Recovery

One underused layer in catering follow-up: the post-event outreach for future business. A client who just had a great experience at their wedding reception is not a future catering client. But their family, friends, and corporate colleagues are. And the corporate client who had an excellent holiday gala is planning next year's holiday gala right now.

The post-event follow-up serves two purposes. First: ensuring the client is genuinely satisfied while the experience is fresh, which also surfaces any feedback that should be addressed. Second: planting the seed for future business or referrals in a moment when the relationship is at its warmest.

The timing: 48-72 hours after the event, when the client is still in the glow of it going well. The content: specific and personal. "Hope the evening went exactly as you'd hoped — we had a great time with your guests. The dessert table feedback was wonderful. Would love to work together again if an occasion comes up, and happy to be a referral for any of your network."

What I Actually Learned

Catering sales lose in one place: the window between the tasting and the signed contract. That window is where the client is comparing, deciding, and choosing based on who showed up most thoughtfully.

The companies that win do three things: they show up consistently in that window, they offer genuine value at each touchpoint rather than just checking in, and they avoid the cheap tactics (scarcity claims, deadline pressure) that every other caterer is using and every client has learned to ignore.

The vendor network layer is the business development piece that sustains this — the referral infrastructure that means the lead flow doesn't depend entirely on whoever happens to Google "catering near me" this month. Building it is slow and relationship-intensive. Maintaining it, once built, is mostly just consistency and a good CRM.
