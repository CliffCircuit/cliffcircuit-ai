Solo law firms book the consult, then go quiet.

A solo estate-planning attorney I know ran a simple audit and found she was converting 28% of her consultations. The other 72% just evaporated — no follow-up, no check-in, nothing. Here's what an AI follow-up agent actually changes, and what the math looks like for a small firm.

A solo attorney I know — estate planning, mid-size city, been practicing 14 years — told me something that stuck. "I've been leaving money on the table for a decade and I didn't even know it until I looked."

She ran a simple audit. Pulled every consultation she'd done in the past 18 months. Compared it against her retained client list. The gap was brutal: she was converting about 28% of consultations to retained clients. The other 72% just... evaporated.

No follow-up call. No check-in email. No "did you have any more questions?" Nothing. They booked a consult, she gave them solid advice for an hour, and then both sides went silent. Some of them hired other attorneys. Some of them did nothing and came back a year later in a worse situation. Some of them probably hired whoever sent one email.

This isn't a discipline problem. She's not lazy. She's busy — like every solo and small-firm attorney I've ever talked to. Between active cases, court dates, client calls, and actually practicing law, following up on cold consults doesn't make the priority list. It's not billable. It's sales work, and most attorneys didn't go to law school to do sales.

But those cold consults are real people with real problems who are probably still trying to solve them. And somebody is going to get that business.

The consultation follow-up problem is specific to law

In most service businesses, the follow-up gap is about speed — the lead went cold because you didn't respond fast enough. That's a different problem. For law firms, especially estate planning, family law, personal injury, and criminal defense, the timeline is longer. People take weeks to decide. They talk to their spouse. They compare options. They think about it and come back.

That extended decision window is actually a huge opportunity — if you're the one who stays present during it. But most small firms aren't. They do the consult, move on to active cases, and assume that if the prospect was serious they'd call back.

Some of them do. Most of them don't, even when they'd been leaning toward hiring you. The friction of being the one to reach out again is just too high, and you've given them no reason to do it.

What the AI agent actually does

The setup I've seen work isn't complicated. After a consultation, an AI agent — in this case running through OpenClaw — sends a sequence of check-ins over the following 10 days. Not sales emails. Not "ready to get started?" pressure. Just useful, specific follow-ups:

Day 1: "Thanks for coming in. Here's that resource I mentioned about the probate process in your state." A link to something genuinely useful. No ask.

Day 3: "Just checking in — do you have any questions from our conversation?" One sentence. Low friction.

Day 7: "I know these decisions take time. No rush — just wanted you to know I'm here if anything came up." That's it.

Day 10: "Circling back one last time. If timing has changed or you'd like to talk through anything else, feel free to reach out." Final touch. After this, the sequence ends.

Four emails in 10 days. None of them are pushy. All of them are from the attorney's own email address (the AI sends on their behalf). Each one takes about 30 seconds to customize before it goes out — the agent drafts it, the attorney approves or sends as-is.

The numbers, honestly

I want to be careful about making specific claims here because every practice area and every firm is different. But the pattern I've seen across a handful of small firms that have tried this:

Conversion rates from consultation to retained client go up. Sometimes significantly. Not because the AI is doing anything magic — because the attorney is now following up at all, consistently, without having to remember to do it or carve time out of a busy day.

The attorney I mentioned at the start ran a three-month test. Consultations that got the AI follow-up sequence converted at roughly double the rate of her historical average. She hired one client who said, honestly, "I'd basically decided to go with someone else and then got your second email and just called." One email. One retained client. That's a real outcome.

The failure case I saw was an attorney who over-automated. He had the agent send six emails in seven days, plus a text, plus a voicemail drop. Prospects started marking emails as spam. One wrote back to say she felt harassed. The volume was wrong. The cadence I described above — four emails in 10 days, no texts, no calls unless the prospect initiates — is the one that consistently works without backfiring.

What the agent needs to get right

The thing that makes or breaks this is personalization. Generic "just checking in" emails don't move anyone. What works is referencing something specific from the consultation — the person's situation, a question they asked, something the attorney said they'd look into.

The way I've seen this work is a brief intake right after the consult: the attorney or their assistant fills in a few fields in a simple form — practice area, what the prospect was dealing with, anything specific to follow up on. The AI uses that to make each email feel like it came from a person who remembers the conversation. Because it did — it's just not the person sending it.

The attorney reviews the drafts in a queue each morning. Takes maybe 5 minutes total. Approves or tweaks. Done. That's the version that scales without feeling automated to the person receiving it.

Practices that actually benefit from this

Not every area of law is a fit. Transactional work where someone calls you, you do the work, you're done — the follow-up window barely exists. But these practice areas tend to have longer decision timelines and see strong lift from a follow-up system:

  • Estate planning — People procrastinate for months. One well-timed email can restart the process.
  • Family law (divorce, custody) — Emotionally complex. People delay. A non-pressured check-in lands well.
  • Personal injury — Competitive market, fast decision windows, but multi-firm shopping is common.
  • Small business / contracts — Business owners are busy. They mean to call back. They don't. A follow-up breaks the inertia.
  • Criminal defense — Urgency is high, which means the window is short, but a fast automated follow-up (within hours, not days) can win the case before someone else gets it.

The part that surprised me

I expected attorneys to push back on this. Lawyers are protective of their brand, their relationships, their professional tone. I thought the idea of "AI sending emails from your inbox" would be a hard no.

Mostly it wasn't. What I heard instead was relief. "I know I should be doing this. I just never do." The AI isn't replacing the attorney's voice — it's making sure their voice actually shows up consistently, instead of only when they have time, which is never.

The one pushback I did hear: some attorneys worried about bar association ethics rules around client communication and AI-generated content. Legitimate concern. The answer is that the attorney reviews and approves every outgoing message before it sends — same as they'd review a letter a paralegal drafted. The agent is a drafting tool, not an autonomous communicator. That distinction matters for compliance and for keeping the attorney in control of what goes out under their name.

Getting started without overbuilding it

The temptation is to build a whole CRM-integrated pipeline with lead scoring and automated intake forms and a dashboard showing conversion rates by practice area. That's fine if you want it, but it's not required to see results.

The minimum version: a simple intake form (Google Form works), a spreadsheet that tracks consultation date and contact info, and an AI agent that reads the form submissions and drafts the four-email sequence. The attorney gets the drafts in a daily digest. Approves or edits. Done.

That version takes a weekend to build and costs maybe /month in AI API costs. If it converts even one additional retained client per month, it paid for itself many times over.

The more built-out version — CRM integration, automatic scheduling, conversion tracking — makes sense once you've proved the concept and want to scale it. But don't wait for the perfect system before starting.

What I'd actually do

If I were a solo attorney or running a small firm, here's how I'd approach it:

Start with a 30-day audit. Pull your consultations from the past year. Compare against retained clients. If your conversion rate is under 40%, you have a follow-up problem worth solving. If it's over 40%, you might still benefit, but the urgency is lower.

Build the minimum version first. Run it for 60 days. Look at the difference in conversion rate between consultations that got the follow-up and your historical baseline. If the lift is real, invest in the more robust setup. If it's not, you've lost a weekend and .

My guess — based on everything I've seen — is the lift will be real. Not because AI is magic. Because consistent, personal follow-up wins clients, and most solo attorneys have never been consistent about it because they've been too busy actually being attorneys.

That's a solvable problem. The tool to solve it exists. The question is whether you'll use it.
